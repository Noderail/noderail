# HCS Research Validation
## Academic Grounding for Human Capacity Science
*Author: Gao Kabubi | Version: 0.2.0 | Date: March 2026*
*Evidence Level: Practitioner-Derived + Literature-Anchored*

## Overview

Human Capacity Science does not invent that trauma matters, that interruptions hurt focus, or that emotional labor drains people. The field claim is:

> "No existing framework adequately unifies these loads into a real-time capacity model that explains performance, crash risk, and recovery needs together."

This document maps each HCS construct to its research anchors — and identifies where HCS extends beyond existing literature.

---

## Construct 1: Emotional Residue

### Research Anchors

**Attention Residue — Sophie Leroy**
When people switch tasks before completing them, part of their attention remains on the prior task. This "residue" impairs performance on the new task. Leroy's work shows the effect is measurable and persistent.
- HCS extends this: Emotional Residue broadens attention residue to include emotional content specifically, and explicitly includes life-context sources (not just task-switching).

**Emotional Labor — Hochschild**
The management of feeling to create a publicly observable facial and bodily display. Knowledge workers perform substantial emotional labor daily — managing how they appear in meetings, with managers, with clients. This costs capacity.

**Rumination Research**
Sustained negative cognition following stressors. Research shows rumination maintains physiological arousal, prevents psychological detachment, and impairs cognitive functioning.

### HCS Extension
Emotional Residue in HCS is explicitly cross-domain (work AND life) and is framed as a measurable capacity signal — not just a psychological observation.

## Construct 2: Cognitive Load

### Research Anchors

**Cognitive Load Theory — Sweller (1988)**
Foundational: cognitive load must be managed to enable learning and performance. Three types: intrinsic (task complexity), extraneous (poor instructional design), germane (schema formation).
- HCS uses the intrinsic/extraneous distinction but applies it to real-time work performance, not learning contexts.

**Working Memory — Baddeley & Hitch**
Working memory capacity is limited (~4 items). When exceeded, performance degrades non-linearly.

**TBRS (Time-Based Resource Sharing) — Barrouillet**
Attention must be continuously refreshed between memory traces. This explains why interruption is so costly — it forces the system to reset at high cognitive cost.

**Task Switching Research — Monsell, Rogers**
Task switching imposes measurable switching cost. Switching between tasks is not free — each switch has cognitive overhead that compounds.

**Decision Fatigue — Baumeister, Tierney**
The quality of decisions deteriorates after extended periods of decision-making. This is cognitive load expressed as temporal degradation.

### HCS Extension
HCS situates Cognitive Load within an integrated real-time model alongside Emotional Residue and Digital Fatigue. Existing CLT research focuses primarily on learning; HCS applies it to sustained work performance.

## Construct 3: Digital Fatigue

### Research Anchors

**Technostress — Tarafdar, Tu, Ragu-Nathan, Ragu-Nathan**
Technology creates stress through: techno-overload, techno-invasion, techno-complexity, techno-insecurity, techno-uncertainty. This is well-documented in knowledge work contexts.

**Attention Fragmentation — Gloria Mark**
Research at UC Irvine shows workers are interrupted every few minutes. Full recovery from interruption takes 23 minutes on average. Workers in interrupted conditions report higher stress, frustration, and time pressure.

**Digital Distraction — Ward et al.**
The mere presence of a smartphone reduces available cognitive capacity — even when the phone is face-down and silent.

**Zoom Fatigue — Bailenson (2021)**
Video conferencing produces specific fatigue: excessive close-up eye contact, reduced mobility, seeing oneself, reduced non-verbal cues. Documents the unique cross-system drain of digital interaction.

### HCS Extension
Digital Fatigue in HCS is defined as cross-system — cognitive, emotional, AND physical simultaneously. Existing research tends to isolate one system. HCS treats the cross-system nature as the defining feature of this construct.

*"We don't have a screen time problem. We have a cognitive leakage problem."*

## Construct 4: Capacity Windows

### Research Anchors

**Intraindividual Variability**
Research demonstrates that cognitive performance varies significantly within individuals across time — often more than between individuals. This is the empirical foundation for Capacity Windows.
- Key finding: same person + same skill + different state → different outcome

**Circadian Performance Rhythms**
Research by Anderson et al., Sievert et al. shows performance on cognitive tasks varies predictably with time of day based on chronotype and circadian phase.

**Mental Bandwidth — Mullainathan & Shafir (Scarcity, 2013)**
Scarcity (financial, time, social) taxes cognitive bandwidth, reducing IQ-equivalent performance on unrelated tasks. This supports the HCS claim that capacity state affects performance regardless of underlying skill.

### HCS Extension
Capacity Windows operationalizes these findings as a real-time diagnostic tool. The innovation is the three-state model (Open/Narrow/Closed) and the connection to specific intervention types per state.

## Construct 5: Capacity-Relative Time

### Research Anchors

**Psychological Time — Ornstein (On the Experience of Time)**
Subjective time varies dramatically based on cognitive and emotional state. An hour under overload and an hour in flow are experientially and functionally distinct.

**Ultradian Rhythms — Kleitman, Peretz Lavie**
Beyond circadian rhythms, humans have ~90-120 minute performance cycles (Basic Rest-Activity Cycles) alternating between higher and lower cognitive performance windows.

### HCS Originality Claim
No existing framework treats time itself as a capacity-relative variable in the context of work performance and productivity systems. Capacity-Relative Time is an original HCS contribution that extends existing psychological time research into a practical framework for scheduling and performance design.

## Construct 6: Human Bandwidth Index (HBI)

### Gap in Literature

> "There is no validated real-time measure of combined cognitive–emotional–digital load."

This is the gap HBI fills. Existing measures address components separately:
- NASA-TLX measures workload post-hoc
- Perceived Stress Scale (PSS) measures stress not capacity
- Mindfulness assessments measure trait not state
- Engagement surveys measure motivation not bandwidth
- No validated tool integrates cognitive + emotional + digital in real-time

### Research Anchors

**NASA Task Load Index (TLX) — Hart & Staveland**
Gold standard post-hoc workload measure. HBI is inspired by TLX but differs: real-time vs. post-hoc, integrated vs. domain-specific, behavioral-proxy vs. self-report-dependent.

**Experience Sampling Method (ESM) — Csikszentmihalyi**
Random interval self-report to capture real-time psychological states. HBI adopts this principle but aims to reduce self-report burden through behavioral inference.

**Biometric Performance Monitoring**
Heart rate variability, EEG measures of cognitive load. HCS acknowledges these but HBI is designed for non-invasive deployment without hardware requirements.

### HCS Originality Claim
HBI is the first proposed integrated real-time measure combining cognitive, emotional, and digital load into a single capacity index. Its key innovation: measuring at the capacity layer rather than the symptom layer.

## Construct 7: Interpersonal Friction

### Research Anchors

**Emotional Labor — Hochschild**
The management of feeling for display purposes. Disproportionately assigned to women and service workers. Sustained emotional labor depletes capacity.

**Stereotype Threat — Steele & Aronson**
The awareness of being at risk of confirming a negative stereotype creates cognitive load and impairs performance. Directly relevant to HCS: this is a measurable capacity drain from social context.

**Incivility Research — Porath & Pearson**
Even mild workplace incivility reduces performance, creativity, and cognitive capacity. Witnesses experience effects even if not directly targeted.

**Code-Switching Research**
Research documents the cognitive and emotional cost of adjusting communication style across social contexts — disproportionate burden on marginalized groups.

**Prove-It-Again Pattern — Cecilia Ridgeway**
Women and minorities face repeated competence challenges that men don't. Each challenge is measurable cognitive and emotional labor.

**Second Shift Research — Hochschild**
Domestic labor falls disproportionately on women after paid work — creating dual extraction patterns invisible to workplace capacity models.

### HCS Originality Claim
HCS is the first framework to explicitly classify Interpersonal Friction as a *capacity variable* rather than a "cultural" or "HR" issue. This makes gender and identity load technically addressable through capacity measurement and system design — not just through training or policy.

## Construct 8: Recovery

### Research Anchors

**Psychological Detachment — Etzion, Eden, Lapidot; Sonnentag & Bayer**
True recovery requires mental detachment from work. Checking email during evening prevents psychological detachment and impairs next-day performance.

**Effort-Recovery Model — Meijman & Mulder**
Recovery is the process of unwinding load-related reactions. If workload demands are too high, full recovery between episodes becomes impossible — leading to accumulation.

**Conservation of Resources Theory — Hobfoll**
People strive to obtain, retain, and protect resources. Capacity threats trigger stress. Resource loss is disproportionately impactful compared to resource gain (loss aversion at the capacity level).

**Sleep Research**
Sleep is the primary macro-recovery mechanism. Sleep deprivation impairs: working memory, emotional regulation, decision quality, executive function — all HBI components.

### HCS Extension
HCS reframes Recovery as a structural condition of systems, not just a personal behavior. This is the key innovation: rather than prescribing recovery as individual discipline, HCS argues that Recovery Conditions must be designed into workflows, schedules, and organizational culture.

---

## Construct 9: Capacity Debt

### Research Anchors

**Allostatic Load — McEwen**
Cumulative wear-and-tear on body systems from chronic stress. The biological analog to Capacity Debt.

**Burnout Research — Maslach, Leiter**
Burnout as gradual resource depletion: emotional exhaustion, depersonalization, reduced accomplishment. HCS reframes burnout as the endpoint of sustained Capacity Debt without adequate recovery.

### HCS Extension
Capacity Debt introduces the *compounding interest* mechanism: debt grows non-linearly when recovery is insufficient, and the cost of not recovering today is paid tomorrow with interest.

---

## Construct 10: Capacity Friction

### Research Anchors

**Business Process Waste — Lean methodology (Womack & Jones)**
Seven forms of waste in production systems. HCS adapts this to human systems: Capacity Friction is the human analog to production waste.

**Bureaucratic Burden Research**
Administrative burden (form-filling, approval seeking, reporting) has measurable costs to focus and executive capacity. Policy extraction in HCS draws from this.

**Coordination Costs — Jensen & Meckling, organizational economics**
Coordination between individuals and teams has cognitive cost. Misaligned systems impose coordination overhead that drains capacity.

## The Unique Gap HCS Fills

### What exists:
- Cognitive load theory (learning context)
- Burnout research (post-hoc, clinical)
- Emotional labor research (sociology)
- Technostress research (IT systems)
- Recovery science (occupational health)
- Intraindividual variability research (psychology)

### What does NOT exist:
- An integrated real-time model unifying cognitive + emotional + digital load
- A predictive capacity measurement tool
- A capacity-based performance framework applicable to knowledge work
- A theory of capacity as system-conditioned (not just individual)
- A measurement ethics framework for capacity data
- A gender-disaggregated capacity model

### HCS claims to fill these gaps.

## Critical Challenges + Responses

1. **"Too broad — human capacity not defined"**
Response: HCS defines capacity precisely as real-time functional bandwidth — measurable through behavioral proxies, not subjective reports alone.

2. **"Methodologically messy"**
Response: Time-series alignment, multilevel modeling, multilevel regression of within-person variation.

3. **"Emotional residue not established"**
Response: Anchored in Leroy's attention residue research + affective interference + rumination literature.

4. **"Digital load not cognitive neuroscience"**
Response: Digital environments modulate the same neural systems (prefrontal executive function, salience networks) documented in cognitive neuroscience.

5. **"Disguised product research"**
Response: Studying fundamental science of capacity variation; DustOff is the applied layer, not the theory.

6. **"Three dissertations in one"**
Response: Integration IS the dissertation — the contribution is the unified model, not each component alone.

7. **"Need neural data for real-time capacity"**
Response: Behavioral neuroscience principles support behavioral proxy measurement. HBI doesn't require EEG.

8. **"Real-time modeling too difficult"**
Response: Staged approach — start with validated self-report + behavioral proxies, build toward continuous modeling.

9. **"Micro-interventions are wellness hacks"**
Response: Reset protocols are based on load reduction and cognitive closure mechanisms, not generic wellness.

10. **"Capacity 'equality' without disaggregation is meaningless"**
Response: Agreed — HCS explicitly requires gender and identity disaggregation in capacity measurement.
