## FRAMEWORK 1: Human Capacity Load Model (HCLM)

**Node ID:** HCS-FW-001

### Core Proposition
Performance is a function of available capacity under load — not talent, discipline, or time alone.

### The Four Load Domains

**1. Cognitive Load**
Mental demands on working memory, attention, and task execution.
- Complex problem solving
- Decision fatigue
- Task switching cost
- Learning demands
- Working memory: ~4 items maximum at once

**2. Emotional Load**
Emotional processing demands that consume psychological bandwidth.
- Conflict and unresolved interactions
- Grief, anxiety, fear of failure
- Emotional labor (managing others' emotions as part of work)
- Life events: illness, caregiving, financial stress, trauma

**3. Digital / Environmental Load**
Strain produced by modern digital work environments.
- Notifications
- Meeting density
- Fragmented tools
- Interruption cycles
- Context switching

**4. Life Context Load**
External life conditions affecting baseline capacity — the layer most frameworks omit.
- Sickness and physical health
- Traumatic events
- Bullying and harassment
- Caregiving responsibilities
- Financial stress
- Sleep deprivation
- Unsafe environments
- Relationship strain

**Why this matters:**
Someone who slept 2 hours because their baby was sick starts the day at 30% capacity. Someone dealing with grief carries constant emotional bandwidth drain. None of this appears in productivity metrics — but all of it affects HBI.

### Load Interaction Dynamics

Loads do not simply accumulate — they interact and compound:
- Emotional residue amplifies cognitive load
- Digital friction accelerates emotional drain
- Workflow friction compounds all three
- Capacity collapse is non-linear: small increases trigger major declines at critical points

### The 3-Layer vs 4-Layer Model

**Full Theory Model (HCS as a field):**
All four loads — cognitive, emotional, digital, life-context

**Operational Product Model (DustOff / HBI measurement):**
Three pillars — cognitive, emotional, digital

Rationale: Life-context loads are captured *downstream* through their effects on the three measurable pillars. HBI doesn't need to ask "are you grieving?" — it detects the elevated Emotional Residue that grief produces.

## FRAMEWORK 2: The Human Capacity Equation

**Node ID:** HCS-FW-002

### The Core Law

```
Usable Capacity(t) = Potential Capacity − Capacity Leakage(t) + Capacity Recovery(t)
```

### Components

**Potential Capacity**
Maximum capacity under ideal conditions. Influenced by: skill, knowledge, experience, motivation, health, training, emotional stability.

Note: Organizations spend most effort here (hiring, training, coaching) — yet this alone doesn't determine performance.

**Capacity Leakage**
Capacity lost before it can be applied. Generated by the Five Friction Categories:
1. Workflow Friction
2. Tool & Information Fragmentation
3. Policy & Administrative Extraction
4. Recovery Deficit
5. Interpersonal Capacity Drain

**Capacity Recovery**
Restoration of usable capacity. Includes both Designed Recovery (intentional structures) and Emergent Recovery (naturally occurring restoration).

### Organizational Extension

```
Organizational Capacity = Aggregate Human Capacity − Systemic Leakage + Systemic Recovery
```

Systemic leakage: coordination failures, duplicated reporting, tool fragmentation, conflicting priorities, cultural pressure patterns.

### Key Principles

1. **Capacity is system-conditioned** — not only individual
2. **Leakage is the operational expression of load** — not just personal experience
3. **Recovery is structural** — depends on environmental design, not just individual effort
4. **Leverage points exist** — small structural changes unlock large capacity gains
5. **Execution depends on usable capacity** — not effort or talent alone

### The Reservoir Model

Think of it visually:
- Potential Capacity fills the reservoir
- Leakage drains it continuously
- Recovery refills it
- Usable capacity = the current water level

Organizations optimize the water supply (hiring better people) while ignoring the leaks. HCS focuses on finding and fixing the leaks.

## FRAMEWORK 3: Capacity Pressure Taxonomy

**Node ID:** HCS-FW-003

### Purpose
Capacity Mapping — the practice of tracing WHERE capacity loss occurs within a system. This transforms HCS from descriptive to diagnostic.

### Five Categories

| Category | What it is | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| Workflow Friction | Structural inefficiencies in work organization | Unclear ownership, duplicated work, excessive handoffs |
| Tool & Information Fragmentation | Digital system fragmentation | Scattered tools, tab overload, information silos |
| Policy Extraction | Administrative burden | Compliance overhead, approval bottlenecks, reporting loops |
| Recovery Deficit | Environments that prevent restoration | Meeting saturation, constant interruption, no transition time |
| Interpersonal Capacity Drain | Social dynamics that erode capacity | Fear-based comms, exclusion, unresolved conflict, bullying |

### Capacity Leverage Points

Not all friction is equal. Some nodes create disproportionate downstream effects:
- Removing redundant approvals
- Consolidating digital tools
- Redesigning meeting architecture
- Resolving interpersonal tensions

Targeting leverage points = large capacity gains from small changes.

## FRAMEWORK 4: Extraction vs Regeneration

**Node ID:** HCS-FW-004

### The System Critique

**Extraction Systems** (most modern work environments):
- Optimize output, ignore capacity
- Assume humans are infinitely available
- React to burnout after damage
- Measure output, not the human generating it

**Regenerative Systems** (what HCS proposes):
- Protect capacity while maintaining performance
- Design for human limits, not despite them
- Detect overload early and intervene
- Measure the capacity layer beneath output

### The Core Critique

*"We built systems that maximize output while ignoring the resource that produces it."*

*"The productivity industry isn't helping you. It's mining you."*

*"Self-care is a $4.5 trillion industry built on the failure to protect capacity in the first place."*

### The Paradigm Shift

| Old Model | HCS Model |
|-----------|-----------|
| Time-based | Capacity-based |
| Output-based | State-based |
| Static human assumption | Dynamic capacity reality |
| Reactive (post-burnout) | Preventive (pre-crash) |
| Individual responsibility | System-conditioned |

## FRAMEWORK 5: The Reset System

**Node ID:** HCS-FW-005

### Philosophy

Recovery must be embedded in workflow, not appended to it. HCS proposes micro-resets as the operational mechanism — small, context-aware capacity restoration moments that interrupt the Doom Loop before crash.

### Three Modes (DustOff Reset)

**Zen Mode**
- Use when: noise, agitation, overstimulation
- Purpose: reduce input, stabilize nervous system
- Resets: Overload Override, Digital Friction Insert, Shutdown Seal

**Flow Mode**
- Use when: fragmented attention, constant switching
- Purpose: protect attention lane, reduce context cost
- Resets: Flow Lane Lock, Tab Tamer, Re-entry Ramp

**Legend Mode**
- Use when: pushing hard, sprint needed
- Purpose: controlled high output with mandatory recovery
- Resets: Legend Recovery, Shutdown Seal

### Signature Reset Library

| Reset | Time | Use When |
|---|---|---|
| Tab Tamer | 45-60s | Tab chaos, open loops everywhere |
| Flow Lane Lock | 2-3min | Can't drop in, constant switching |
| Re-entry Ramp | 60-120s | Interrupted, can't restart |
| Meeting Detox | 90s | Post-call fog, emotional hangover |
| Open Loop Dump | 30-60s | Mind rehearsing unfinished things |
| Context Switch Cleanse | 2-3min | Task transitions feel expensive |
| Overload Override | 2-4min | Urgency spike, doom-scroll pull |
| Digital Friction Insert | 30-90s | Compulsive checking, autopilot |
| Legend Recovery | 3-6min | Pushed hard, feeling brittle |
| Shutdown Seal | 2-5min | End-of-day buzzing brain |

### Core Principles

- Reset first. Decide second.
- Capacity first. Strategy second.
- If you're switching, you're overloaded.
- This is not a motivation problem. It's a capacity timing problem.

## FRAMEWORK 6: HCS Measurement Architecture

**Node ID:** HCS-FW-006

### The Measurement Claim

*"The breakthrough claim of Human Capacity Science is not only that capacity matters. It is that capacity can be measured well enough to become a usable operating variable in human systems."*

### Four-Layer Stack

**Layer 1 — Latent Construct**
Usable Human Capacity (the variable we care about, not always directly observable)

**Layer 2 — Observable Inputs**

*Behavioral Signals:*
- App/tab switching frequency
- Focus duration
- Re-entry delays after interruption
- Incomplete task loop accumulation
- Typing latency
- Interaction burst patterns

*Self-Report Signals:*
- Perceived overload
- Emotional residue (felt)
- Clarity of thinking
- Readiness to engage

*Contextual Signals:*
- Meeting density
- Workflow complexity
- Communication volume
- Policy burden

*Recovery Signals:*
- Breaks taken
- Resets completed
- Task closure rate

**Layer 3 — Inference Engine**
Signals → estimated capacity state via:
- Time-series modeling
- Bayesian approaches
- Behavioral pattern recognition
- Leakage rate calculation

**Layer 4 — Representation**
Capacity expressed as:
- Human Bandwidth Index (0-100)
- Capacity Zone (Green/Yellow/Red)
- Capacity Window Status (Open/Narrow/Closed)
- Capacity Risk Level

### Signal Constructs

| Signal | Definition |
|---|---|
| Re-entry Friction | Difficulty resuming work after interruption |
| Focus Stability | Consistency of attention across work blocks |
| Recovery Engagement | Interaction with recovery opportunities |
| Overload Clustering | Patterns of overload around specific events/rhythms |

### Measurement Principles

1. **State-based, not identity-based** — measures current conditions, not fixed labels
2. **Dynamic** — single snapshots are weak; trends matter
3. **Multi-signal** — no single metric captures capacity adequately
4. **Contextual** — signals mean different things in different environments
5. **Ethical** — capacity data cannot become performance weaponry

### The Ethical Boundary

Capacity measurement IS for:
- Seeing invisible load
- Improving execution conditions
- Protecting capacity
- Enabling fairer performance interpretation

Capacity measurement is NOT for:
- Ranking human worth
- Punishing low-capacity states
- Forcing people to prove depletion
- Surveillance theater
