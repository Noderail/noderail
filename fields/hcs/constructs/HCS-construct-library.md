## CONSTRUCT 1: Emotional Residue
**Node ID:** HCS-C-001

**Canonical Definition:**
The unprocessed emotional weight carried forward from one event, interaction, or task into the next. When feelings from a previous experience have not been acknowledged or resolved, they occupy cognitive and emotional bandwidth in subsequent contexts — reducing available capacity without the individual being aware of the source.

**Origin (in Gao's words):**
*"When you have unprocessed feelings which we carry from one event to another."*

**Mechanism:**
- Open emotional loops from prior interactions remain active
- Emotional content contaminates working memory
- Previous tasks, meetings, or life events continue running in the background
- Sources: difficult meetings, unresolved conversations, personal life stress, social media exposure, internal self-talk

**Expression:**
- Reduced clarity in the next task
- Slower thinking and decision-making
- Irritability without clear cause
- Inability to engage fully with the present task
- *"You finished the meeting. The meeting didn't finish with you."*

**Important:** Emotional residue is not limited to work. It includes life → work carryover, work → work carryover, and digital → emotional spillover.

**Research Anchors:** Attention Residue (Sophie Leroy), Emotional Labor (Hochschild)

**HCS Position:** Core signal in HBI calculation. Source of capacity leakage. Addressed through Meeting Detox and Open Loop Dump reset protocols.

## CONSTRUCT 2: Cognitive Load
**Node ID:** HCS-C-002

**Canonical Definition:**
The total weight of information the mind is actively holding, processing, or attempting to retain at any given moment. When cognitive load exceeds available capacity, processing slows, errors increase, and the ability to take in new information degrades — even when the individual feels they are still functioning normally.

**Origin (in Gao's words):**
*"The weight our minds carry, what our minds is able to process or retain information."*

**Mechanism:**
- Working memory can hold approximately 4 items at once
- Task switching inflates cognitive switching cost
- Multitasking compounds interference
- Competing priorities exhaust executive control

**Expression:**
- Brain fog
- Errors in familiar tasks
- Reduced execution quality
- Difficulty choosing a next step
- *"Your brain is like a phone with 50 apps running — it slows down and eventually crashes."*

**Research Anchors:** Cognitive Load Theory (Sweller), Working Memory (Baddeley), TBRS models

**HCS Position:** Core pillar of HBI. Addressed through Flow Lane Lock and Tab Tamer reset protocols.

## CONSTRUCT 3: Digital Fatigue
**Node ID:** HCS-C-003

**Canonical Definition:**
The cumulative state that emerges from prolonged engagement with digital tools and environments. It goes beyond physical tiredness — manifesting as eye strain, neck pain, and postural tension — because it simultaneously taxes cognitive, emotional, and physical capacity. Digital Fatigue is not a single-system response. It is a cross-system drain that degrades the ability to think, feel, and function — often without the individual recognising the source.

**Origin (in Gao's words):**
*"The effect of overuse or indulgence with digital products... it is beyond tired because it ends up affecting other capabilities — taxing on emotions, cognitive, and physical."*

**What makes this construct original:**
Most fatigue research picks one system. Digital Fatigue in HCS hits all three simultaneously — cognitive, emotional, and physical. That cross-system nature is the HCS contribution.

**Mechanism:**
- Notifications fragment sustained attention
- Tab overload creates open cognitive loops
- Context switching prevents cognitive reset
- Always-on environments eliminate recovery
- *"Every tab is a promise your brain is still trying to keep."*

**Expression:**
- Eye tiredness
- Neck and shoulder pain
- Mental fragmentation
- Reduced executive control
- Passive consumption loops
- *"Screen time is the wrong metric. Open loops is the right one."*

**Research Anchors:** Technostress (Tarafdar), Attention Span research (Gloria Mark), Interruption research

**HCS Position:** Core pillar of HBI. Generates capacity leakage via Tool Fragmentation and Recovery Deficit pressures.

## CONSTRUCT 4: Capacity Windows
**Node ID:** HCS-C-004

**Canonical Definition:**
The available state of a person's ability to perform at any given moment — shaped by the combined influence of Digital Fatigue, Emotional Residue, Cognitive Load, and interpersonal friction. Capacity Windows exist on a spectrum.

**Origin (in Gao's words):**
*"The point in one's being to perform tasks either at its optimal or minimal output — how much one can do in that given time in relation to digital fatigue, emotional and cognitive load, and the different frictions surrounding them, especially interpersonal friction."*

**Three States:**
- **Open** — optimal output available. Full engagement, clear thinking, best performance possible.
- **Narrow** — reduced output. Functioning but below potential. Tasks take longer, quality drops, errors increase.
- **Closed** — minimal or no productive output. Continuing to work in this state compounds capacity damage rather than producing meaningful results.

**Key Insight:**
Performance failure is often not a skill problem or a motivation problem. It is a timing problem — the right task attempted in the wrong Capacity Window.

*"The question isn't can they perform — it's when can they perform."*
*"You're not inconsistent. Your capacity fluctuates. The expectations don't."*

**Research Anchors:** Intraindividual variability research, Attention rhythm studies (Gloria Mark)

**HCS Position:** The operational output of HBI measurement. Determines intervention timing.

## CONSTRUCT 5: Capacity-Relative Time
**Node ID:** HCS-C-005
**Status:** Original — emerged March 19, 2026

**Canonical Definition:**
In Human Capacity Science, time is not treated as a neutral, uniform resource. An hour of open capacity and an hour of closed capacity are not equivalent units. Time in HCS is always measured in relation to the four core factors — Cognitive Load, Emotional Residue, Digital Fatigue, and Capacity Windows — because the same clock-time produces fundamentally different outputs depending on the capacity state of the person within it.

**The implication:** Scheduling, productivity, and performance measurement systems that ignore capacity state are measuring the wrong variable.

**Origin (in Gao's words):**
*"In human capacity we don't talk of time in its generic sense but in relation to the four factors we mentioned."*

**What makes this construct original:**
Every existing productivity and performance framework assumes time is neutral. HCS is the first framework to assert that time is capacity-relative — that an hour means something fundamentally different depending on the state of the human within it.

*"Time management assumes you have something left to manage."*
*"Same 24 hours. Different capacity. That's why some days you're a machine and some days you can barely reply to an email."*

**HCS Position:** Theoretical foundation for HCS's critique of time-based productivity systems. Supports the argument for capacity-based scheduling.

## CONSTRUCT 6: Human Bandwidth Index (HBI)
**Node ID:** HCS-C-006

**Canonical Definition:**
A real-time measurement of a person's available capacity — reflecting what they can take on, process, and deliver at any given moment.

**Core Pillars:**
1. **Cognitive Load** — the weight of information the mind is currently holding
2. **Emotional Residue** — unprocessed emotional weight carried forward from previous experiences
3. **Digital Fatigue** — the cross-system drain from prolonged digital engagement

**Extended Understanding:**
The three pillars are themselves shaped by broader life factors — sleep quality, traumatic events, interpersonal friction, physical health, and environmental stressors. HBI does not measure these directly. Instead it captures their *downstream effect* on capacity. Poor sleep shows up as elevated Cognitive Load. Unresolved conflict shows up as Emotional Residue. A difficult life event shows up across all three pillars simultaneously.

This makes HBI a practical real-time signal without requiring invasive data collection about a person's private life. The measurement stays at the capacity layer — not the cause layer.

**What HBI does NOT measure:**
Skill, intelligence, motivation, character, or worth.

**Measurement approach:**
- Behavioral signals (tab switching, focus duration, interaction patterns)
- Optional self-report (quick state check-ins)
- Inferred from context (meeting density, workflow load)

**Score states:**
- Green (75-100): Capacity available — optimal window
- Yellow (50-74): Capacity compressed — narrow window
- Red (<50): Capacity depleted — closed window

**Origin note:** HBI replaced ELI as the core metric. "Human Bandwidth" was chosen because bandwidth is already a universal term — in life it means capacity to handle demands; in technology it means strength and flow of connection.

**HCS Position:** Primary measurement output of the field. DustOff Reset is the first system operationalizing HBI.

## CONSTRUCT 7: Interpersonal Friction
**Node ID:** HCS-C-007

**Canonical Definition:**
The capacity erosion caused by negative interactions with other people. Interpersonal Friction creates delay, heaviness, and a degraded ability to perform — not because the person lacks skill or motivation, but because the interaction itself has consumed bandwidth.

It manifests on a spectrum — from subtle interpersonal tension and microaggressions to overt forms including bullying, name-calling, sexism, and racism. Regardless of intensity, all forms of Interpersonal Friction share a common mechanism: they generate Emotional Residue, elevate Cognitive Load, and narrow or close the Capacity Window of the person experiencing them.

**Origin (in Gao's words):**
*"The erosion caused by interaction with other people which results in negative impact — it can cause delay, heaviness and erodes one's ability to perform at their optimal capacity. This can present as bullying, name calling, sexism, racism etc."*

**Why this matters:**
Most productivity and performance frameworks ignore Interpersonal Friction entirely. HCS names it as a capacity variable — which is both a scientific statement and a political one. The bullying, the racism, the sexism — these are not "soft" issues. They are measurable capacity drains.

Interpersonal Friction is particularly significant in workplace contexts because it is often invisible to those not experiencing it — yet its capacity cost is real, measurable, and compounding.

**Types:**
- Unresolved conflict and tension
- Fear-based communication
- Exclusion or favoritism
- Intimidation and power dynamics
- Self-censorship under threat
- Unclear expectations creating anxiety
- Meeting dynamics where some voices are suppressed

**Gender dimension:**
Women — especially women of color — carry disproportionate Interpersonal Friction load through: the likeability tax, the prove-it-again pattern, code-switching costs, safety load, emotional labor, and the motherhood penalty. This makes HBI scores non-comparable without disaggregation by gender and other identity factors.

*"Capacity isn't distributed equally. Systems deplete women faster — then blame them for being depleted."*

**HCS Position:** Fifth capacity pressure category. Part of Life Context Load in HBI extended model. Critical for organizational capacity diagnostics.

## CONSTRUCT 8: Recovery
**Node ID:** HCS-C-008

**Canonical Definition:**
The pathways and opportunities — whether deliberately designed or naturally occurring — that enable the regeneration and restoration of Human Bandwidth. Recovery is not passive rest. It is the active process by which Cognitive Load is reduced, Emotional Residue is processed, and Digital Fatigue is reversed — returning the Capacity Window toward an open state.

**Origin (in Gao's words):**
*"Opportunities/paths which can be established or exist to ensure regeneration and restoration of HBI."*

**Critical principle:**
Recovery is not optional in HCS. It is structural. Without recovery pathways built into environments, workflows, and cultures, HBI degrades progressively — until capacity collapse becomes inevitable.

**The absence of recovery is not neutral. It is accumulative damage.**

**Two types:**
- **Designed Recovery** — intentional structures built into work and life: breaks, transitions, boundaries, rituals, reset protocols
- **Emergent Recovery** — naturally occurring moments of restoration: genuine connection, flow states, time in nature, sleep, task completion

**Recovery Conditions:**
Recovery depends on environmental conditions, not just individual discipline.
- Meeting architecture
- Schedule design and sequencing
- Interruption patterns
- Organizational expectations
- Tool design

Without supportive Recovery Conditions, personal recovery strategies fail.

*"The weekend isn't recovery. It's triage."*
*"Vacation doesn't fix a system designed to deplete you."*

**Research Anchors:** Psychological Detachment (Sonnentag), Recovery Science

**HCS Position:** Counter-force to Capacity Leakage in the Human Capacity Equation.

## CONSTRUCT 9: Capacity Debt
**Node ID:** HCS-C-009

**Canonical Definition:**
The accumulated gap between capacity required by work and life demands and capacity restored through recovery, boundaries, and focus hygiene. When this gap persists, interest compounds as: slower thinking, emotional spillover, doom-scrolling, decision fatigue, irritability, avoidant behaviors, and eventually crash.

**Analogy:**
Think of capacity like a financial balance sheet:
- **Assets:** sleep quality, breaks taken, deep-work blocks, recovery rituals
- **Liabilities:** meetings without breaks, tab chaos, after-hours work, interrupted sessions
- **Interest:** how fast the debt compounds (unique per person)

*"You came back from PTO and felt tired by Tuesday. That's not you. That's debt collection."*
*"Burnout isn't a breakdown. It's a balance due."*

**HCS Position:** The accumulative consequence of sustained Capacity Leakage without sufficient Recovery.

## CONSTRUCT 10: Capacity Friction
**Node ID:** HCS-C-010

**Canonical Definition:**
Any external condition that increases the effort required to think, decide, or execute. Capacity Friction is the system-level expression of load — it explains how organizational structures and environments generate capacity leakage.

**Five Categories:**

**1. Workflow Friction**
Structural inefficiencies in how work is organized.
- Unclear task sequencing
- Redundant processes
- Excessive handoffs
- Meeting fragmentation
- Coordination failures

**2. Tool & Information Fragmentation**
Friction from scattered digital systems.
- Tab overload
- Multiple disconnected tools
- Information silos
- Version confusion
- Constant context switching between platforms

**3. Policy & Administrative Extraction**
Capacity drained by administrative burden.
- Compliance overhead
- Reporting loops
- Rigid approval chains
- Bureaucratic rules

**4. Recovery Deficit**
Environments that prevent capacity restoration.
- Back-to-back meetings
- No transition time between tasks
- Always-on communication norms
- Schedules with no white space

**5. Interpersonal Capacity Drain**
Social conditions that erode capacity.
- See Construct 7 (Interpersonal Friction)

**Key Insight:**
*"Leakage is the operational expression of load."*

The Human Capacity Equation gains power when you understand that leakage is not random — it is generated by identifiable friction points in systems. This moves HCS from descriptive to diagnostic.

**HCS Position:** The five-category taxonomy of Capacity Leakage. Foundation for Capacity Mapping and the ECI (Enterprise Capacity Intelligence) platform.

## The Doom Loop

**Definition:** A reinforcing cycle where the three core loads amplify each other:

```
Digital Fatigue
    → Emotional Residue
        → Cognitive Overload
            → Increased Digital Susceptibility
                → Digital Fatigue (repeat)
```

*"Digital load amplifies emotional residue. Emotional residue increases cognitive load. Cognitive overload increases susceptibility to digital distraction."*

This is why single-point interventions (just meditate, just put your phone down) are insufficient. The loop requires system-level interruption.
