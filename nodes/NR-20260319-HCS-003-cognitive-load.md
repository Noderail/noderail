# Cognitive Load

**Construct Type:** Measurement Node  
**Field:** Human Capacity Science  
**Version:** v0.1.0 · Draft  
**Evidence Level:** Practitioner-Derived  
**Permitted Use:** Open Exploration  

---

## Definition

The total weight of information the mind is actively holding, processing, or attempting to retain at any given moment. When cognitive load exceeds available capacity, processing slows, errors increase, and the ability to take in new information degrades — even when the individual feels they are still functioning normally.

---

## Why This Construct Matters

Cognitive Load is the most studied of the HCS core constructs, with roots in cognitive psychology and instructional design (Sweller, 1988). HCS adopts and extends this construct to address modern work conditions: the compounding load created by digital tool fragmentation, persistent notifications, meeting-to-meeting scheduling, and the expectation of simultaneous availability across multiple channels.

In HCS, Cognitive Load is not treated as a static task property — it is treated as a dynamic, measurable state that fluctuates across the workday and interacts with Emotional Residue and Digital Fatigue to determine real-time capacity.

The key HCS insight: individuals often cannot accurately self-assess when they have exceeded optimal cognitive load. The sense of "still functioning" persists while output quality silently degrades.

---

## Construct Dimensions

| Dimension | Description |
|---|---|
| **Intrinsic load** | Complexity inherent to the task or information itself |
| **Extraneous load** | Load generated by environmental demands unrelated to the task (interruptions, tool-switching, notifications) |
| **Germane load** | Load invested in understanding and integrating new information |
| **Stack depth** | Number of open, unresolved cognitive loops being held simultaneously |
| **Subjective awareness** | The degree to which the individual recognises their current load state |

## Measurement Approach (v0.1)

### Method: Self-Report Pulse (Privacy-First)

**Sample probe items (5-point scale: Not at all → Completely):**
- "My mind feels full right now — like there's no more room."
- "I'm holding too many open tasks or decisions at once."
- "I keep losing my place or forgetting what I was just doing."
- "Taking in new information right now feels effortful."
- "I feel like I'm operating several things in my head simultaneously."

**Scoring:**
- **0–1:** Low load — capacity available for complex work
- **2:** Moderate load — functioning, some bandwidth constraints
- **3:** High load — recommend task offloading or structured break
- **4–5:** Overload state — errors likely, new input unlikely to be retained; reset intervention indicated

### Complementary Signals (Non-Invasive)
- Self-reported number of open task contexts
- Voluntary context-switch count (self-logged)
- Pre-task focus readiness rating
- Meeting density in prior 90-minute window

## Relationship to Other HCS Constructs

| Construct | Relationship |
|---|---|
| **Emotional Residue** | Residue adds to effective cognitive load; the two compounds are additive, not independent |
| **Digital Fatigue** | Digital Fatigue degrades the capacity to manage cognitive load efficiently |
| **Capacity Windows** | Cognitive Load is a primary determinant of whether a Capacity Window is Open, Narrow, or Closed |
| **Human Bandwidth Index (HBI)** | Cognitive Load is a core HBI pillar |
| **Capacity-Relative Time** | High cognitive load renders clock time functionally compressed — less gets processed per unit |

## HCS Extension of the Construct

Traditional Cognitive Load Theory was developed primarily in instructional contexts. HCS extends it to address:

- **Tool fragmentation load:** The cognitive tax of switching between digital environments (Slack, email, project tools, browser tabs)
- **Availability load:** The load generated by the expectation of constant responsiveness
- **Decision queue load:** The accumulation of deferred decisions as a form of background processing
- **Meeting carryover load:** The unresolved action items and interpersonal dynamics that persist from back-to-back meetings

These extensions are practitioner-derived and represent the v0.1 HCS framing. Empirical validation is a roadmap item.

---

## Intervention Signals

When Cognitive Load is measured at high or overload levels:

- **Micro reset:** Brain dump / externalisation (write everything currently held in working memory)
- **Meso reset:** Single-task block with notifications off; structured break
- **Macro reset:** Task triage and queue reduction; deliberate context closure
- **Design intervention:** Reduce incoming load sources (meeting density, notification volume, tool count)

---

## Ethics and Safeguards

- Cognitive Load scores are the individual's data — not the organisation's
- Scores must never be used to assess "how hard someone is working"
- High load states must not trigger disciplinary responses
- Aggregated anonymised load patterns may inform workload design and scheduling policy
- Measurement frequency must be determined by the individual, not mandated

## Validity Notes (v0.1)

Cognitive Load as a general construct has strong empirical support in adjacent fields (cognitive psychology, instructional design, human factors). The HCS-specific framing — particularly the extensions for digital work contexts — is practitioner-derived and draws on 108+ customer discovery interviews. HCS-specific validation studies are planned for v0.2.

**Evidence base:** Adjacent peer-reviewed literature (Sweller 1988, 2011; Kahneman 1973), practitioner observation, qualitative interview synthesis  
**Validation status:** Construct adopted from validated literature; HCS-specific extensions are pre-empirical
